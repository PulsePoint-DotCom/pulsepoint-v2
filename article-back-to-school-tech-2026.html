Back-to-School Tech 2026: The Best Laptops, Tablets, and Gadgets Under $500

July 20, 2026 · 5 min read

August is closer than you think — and if you're a student or parent starting to think about back-to-school gear, the good news is that solid tech has never been cheaper. The bad news? There's also more garbage masquerading as "student laptops" than ever before. We're sorting through the noise.

The Standout Laptop: Lenovo IdeaPad Flex 5i (Gen 12)

At around $429, the Lenovo IdeaPad Flex 5i continues to be the default recommendation for students who need something that can handle essays, research, video calls, and the occasional streaming session. The 14-inch touchscreen flips into tablet mode, the keyboard is comfortable for all-day use, and it now ships with a 13th-gen Intel Core i3 or AMD Ryzen 3 option — both plenty fast for the average student workload.

Battery life sits around 10 hours with typical use, which means most of a school day without hunting for an outlet. For a sub-$500 machine, that's still hard to beat in 2026.

The Best Tablet for Notes: Samsung Galaxy Tab S9 FE

At $449 with the S Pen included, the Galaxy Tab S9 FE is the best Android tablet deal for students who prefer handwriting notes over typing. The S Pen is pressure-sensitive, magnetically attaches to the back of the tablet, and has virtually zero lag — something you can't say about most cheap styluses.

It also runs DeX mode, which gives you a desktop-like interface when you connect a Bluetooth keyboard, turning it into a quasi-laptop for heavier work sessions.

The Budget Wonder: Acer Chromebook Plus 515

Chromebooks have come a long way. The Acer Chromebook Plus 515 ($379) runs ChromeOS with local AI features baked in — including live captioning, voice typing, and a built-in reading mode. For students who live in Google Docs, Sheets, and Google Meet, this might be the only machine they ever need. It boots in under 5 seconds, gets 12 hours of battery, and weighs just 3.4 pounds.

What to Skip

Anything marketed as a "gaming laptop" under $600 is usually a false economy — the GPU will be outdated within a year, and the build quality rarely survives daily commuting. If gaming matters to you, save up another $200 or consider a used machine from last year's lineup.

Also skip the extended warranties at big-box stores. Most student laptops come with a 1-year manufacturer warranty that's sufficient for the lifespan of the device.
